The Culture of Seal Cutting

By Guo Fuxiang

A time-honored form of art, Chinese seal cutting has never declined despite a millennia-old history. During the period, the materials used for seal cutting have also undergone continuous diversification. The most significant change was the discovery and utilization of seal stones.

It is said that Wang Mian, a poet and painter of the late Yuan Dynasty, was the first to make seals with stone. In Revised Drafts in Seven Categories, Lang Ying, a noted scholar of the Ming Dynasty, wrote: “People used to make seals in bronze until the late Yuan Dynasty, when Wang Mian from Huiji began to cut seals with Huaru stone. Today, all seal cutters favor Dongming stone produced in Chuzhou Prefecture, which is glossy and adorable.” Lang Ying lived during the reign of Emperor Jiajing (1522-

1566) of the Ming Dynasty, and his description indicates that stone materials had already gained popularity in seal cutting at that time. As a matter of fact, the popularity of stone seaks was largely attributed to the seal-cutting practices of scholars in areas south of the Yangtze River. In Legends of Seal Cutters, Zhou Lianggong, an eminent scholar in the late Qing Dynasty, vividly recounts the story of famous Ming-Dynasty seal cutter Wen Peng discovering Dongguangdong stone and using it to cut seals, providing important evidence for research into the rise of stone seal materials. The following is from Zhou’s book:

I heard that when he served as a supervisor at Nanjing Imperial Academy, Wen Peng took a sedan chair to cross the Xihong Bridge, where he saw a donkey carrying two baskets of stones, followed by an aged man shouldering another two baskets. The old man began quarreling with a merchant at the market. Wen stopped and asked [what had caused the argument]. “The merchant promised to buy the stones collected from the river,” the old man replied.
