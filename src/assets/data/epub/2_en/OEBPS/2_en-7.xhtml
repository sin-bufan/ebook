“I asked him to pay a small amount for the labor of the donkey and the porters, but he refused. Thus, our quarrel disturbed you.” Wen gazed at the old man and said: “Don’t quarrel with him. I’ll buy these stones and pay you double the labor cost.” Wen, thus, got the four baskets of stones. When unpacked, he found that most were what are now known as Dongguangdong stone, with even the inferior ones were Laokeng stone. One time, Hong Zhong, the Minister of War in Nanjing, visited Wen and was impressed by the stones. Previously, Wen had made seals out of ivory. [The Minister] wrote inscriptions by himself and then asked Li Wenfu from Jinling (namely, Nanjing) to carve them on seals. After getting these stones, Wen no longer made ivory seals. Hong got hundreds of such stones from Wen, and asked He Zhuchen to engrave inscriptions by Wen and make them seals. From then on, the Dongguangdong stone gained popularity in seal-cutting practices. Before beeswax was used for jewelry, Jinling people used the Dongguangdong stone to craft floral or insect-shaped ornaments for women, and those who purchased the stone also used it for this purpose, instead of making seals.
