Most works by famous Qing-Dynasty seal cutters only have ordinary knobs. Thus, we can conclude that seal knob carving was already separate from seal cutting to become a form of art with unique appreciation value and an independent genre of cultural collectibles. In this period, seal knobs crafted by Dong Cangmen, Yang Yuxuan, and Zhao Shangjun were popular with collectors. Due to cherishing the very nature of stone materials and the seal knobs themselves, collectors usually did not carve inscriptions on them. Perhaps, they thought it unnecessary considering that seal knobs were already artifacts deserving to be passed down through the generations. Such a practice peaked in the late Qing Dynasty and the early Republic of China period (1912-1949). Today, we can still find many uninscribed seals with carved knobs tracing back to the Republic of China period. People even pay more attention to the

aesthetic value of seal knobs than seals themselves. Like other art genres such as calligraphy, seal knobs were bestowed with independent value and attraction once their aesthetic value overweighed practical value.

In the Republic of China period, art genres were increasingly diversified due to the collapse of royal majesty. People’s preferences for art changed accordingly. As a result, seal knobs turned from scrupulous and majestic to elegant and exquisite. Of course, this was partially because of the decline in national strength and the scarcity of stone materials. Tianhuang stones and Jingpindou stone resources were gradually exhausted in the Republic of China period, coupled with political turmoil and economic recession. In this context, it was hard to produce masterpieces like those in the heydays of the Qing Dynasty. Due to the scarcity of stone materials, most seal knobs made at the time are small in size, and to complement the congenial scarcity of stone materials, artisans developed exquisite, skillful carving techniques and craftsmanship.
