Formerly used to carve ornaments, Dongguangdong stone became a material particularly good for seal cutting. Despite the fact that there were some accidental factors, such a change was an inevitable consequence of the popularity of seal cutting amongst scholars in areas south of the Yangtze River, exerting far-reaching influence on the evolution of Chinese seal cutting. Before stones were adopted, most scholars wrote inscriptions on seals by themselves, and then asked experienced sculptors to carve the seals. The standards for judging the carving lay in how well it could retain the style of the original inscriptions. The situation changed with the growing popularity of stone seals: Scholars began to inscribe and carve seals by themselves, which enabled them to give full play to their artistic talent and actualized the real ‘scholarly seal cutting.’ Moreover, this marked an epoch in the evolution of seal materials. Thanks to the discovery and wide utilization of seal stones, seal cutters obtained a kind of material that seems inexhaustible in supply and is easy to carve.
