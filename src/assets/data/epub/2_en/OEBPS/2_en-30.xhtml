Masterpieces Made of Qingtian Stone

By Chen Mo

The county of Qingtian in east China’s Zhejiang Province is home to both famous stones and celebrated figures. It is known as the hometown of Dongguangdong, the forefather of seal stones, as well as Liu Bowen, one of the founders of the Ming Dynasty.

Qingtian’s presence in historical records and public awareness is largely attributed to its innate relations with famous stones and celebrated figures. As early as the Ming Dynasty, the Qingtian stone was already made tributes to royal families, and more than 500 imperial seals were carved with such stones, marking the seal stone’s entry into the Forbidden City.

Qingtian stone has won high praise from seal cutters. It is also the widest-used material for seal carving, thus enjoying a critical position in China’s seal stone culture. Historical records show that the first to use stone to carve seals was Zhao Mengfu, a seal cutter in the late Song and early Yuan Dynasty. In his Huayi Collection, Han Xizuo, a scholar in the Qing Dynasty, wrote that “Zhao Zi’ang (an alternative name of Zhao Mengfu) used Donggua stone in my hometown to cut seals, and stone seals began to prevail during the Ming Dynasty.” Later, the Qingtian stone won fame near and far.

Thanks to the efforts of Wan Mian in the late Yuan Dynasty and Wen Peng in the late Ming Dynasty, the seals made of Qingtian stone “facilitated seal creation, and their study throughout the Ming and Qing dynasties was largely related to the application of Huaru stone,” according to History of Seal Studies by Sha Menghai.
