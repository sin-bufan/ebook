Zhao Mengfu’s occasional attempt to cut seals with the Qingtian stone eventually inspired a revolution in seal materials. The spread of the seal stone put an end to the era of bronze seals that had lasted for 20 centuries. Throughout China’s brilliant seal-cutting history, the Qingtian stone has written down an important chapter with its epoch-making contributions, which gradually created the most famous seal cutters organization both at home and abroad―the Xileng Seal-cutting Society.

The revolution in seal materials created a new historical phase of development for Qingtian. According to the Xileng Seal-Cutting Society, the later four seal cutters made 344 seals, of which 216 were carved using Qingtian stone, accounting for 70 percent of the total. Qingtian serves as a mainstream seal stone, which is determined

by its well-known characteristics such as “translucent texture, elegant colors, moderate hardness and carving ease.” Thus, seals carved with such stone can fully demonstrate the charm of calligraphy. Of all categories of Qingtian stone, the most famous include Dongguandong as translucent and precious as jade, Huangjinyao with its gold color, Lanxing as brilliant as the stars, and the marvelous Longdan stone. Wu Rizhang, a seal cutter in the Ming Dynasty, once explained: “Qingtian stone features moderate texture, and calligraphers can easily carve their inscriptions on it. Its precious nature lies in the fact the stone can perfectly showcase the mind of the creator.” Ding Jing, also an eminent seal cutter, deemed that the older a Qingtian stone is, the better it can be used for seal cutting. Huang Yi, one of the Eight Famous Xileng Seal Cutters believed the stone was “easy to carve”. Zhang Zhiqian claimed that “Qingtian stone is unparalleled for its hardness, translucence, and elegance.” Since then, it has prevailed throughout the world, especially amongst scholars, as the most ideal material for seal-cutting.
