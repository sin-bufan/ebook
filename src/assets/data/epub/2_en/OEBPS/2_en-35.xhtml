Changhua Chicken-Blood Stone Carving involves unique techniques and is reputed to be a “national quintessence.” In 2007, it was inscribed into the National Intangible Cultural Heritage List. Both the Changhua stone and the Changhua Chicken-Blood Stone Carving are of high historical, cultural and artistic value.

Historical Value: In the late Yuan Dynasty, painter Wang Mian began to use the Changhua stone. Later, famous scholars, including Ming Dynasty seal cutter Wen Peng further explored the stone’s qualities. During the Ming and Qing dynasties, it had become a rare seal stone popular among emperors, business tycoons, and celebrated figures. Among seals serving as certificates of authority, imperial seals were the most dignified. Emperor Kangxi once carved a square seal in chicken-

blood stone, decorated with a knob of cloud dragon patterns, which bore the words translated as “Keep in Mind both Danger and Peace”–an admonition that the emperor intended to share responsibility with his officials. Besides Kangxi, other Qing emperors such as Yongzheng, Qianlong, Xianfeng, Tongzhi, and Xuantong all used Changhua Chicken-Blood Stone to make their seals. The seal of Emperor Qianlong, bearing the words “Qianlong Chenhan,” was presented by Master Wenyuan, an abbot of Chanyuan Temple in western Lin’an City’s Tianmu Mountain, as an imperial gift. Reputed a “national treasure,” the seal is now part of the collection of the Palace Museum in Beijing.

Cultural Value: The chicken-blood stone is cherished by seal cutters and collectors due to its unique beauty and ease of carving, hence its nickname “queen of seal stones”. Renowned painter and seal cutter Qi Baishi once made two seals for Chairman Mao Zedong, now preserved in the Central Archives.
