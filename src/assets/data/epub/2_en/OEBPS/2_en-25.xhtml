Due to different mineral contents and mineralizing conditions, chicken-blood stones vary in hardness. Those with high content of silicon are too hard to carve, while those with pink base are too soft and few seal cutters like it. Ding Jing, a representative of the Zhejiang Seal-cutting School, once said, “such stones are as tender as women, just as a steed runs on soft cotton so that it cannot gallop freely.” In fact, many seal cutters misunderstand the Changhua stone, even more than the Shoushan stone. Perhaps the reason is that Changhua places too much priority on its chicken-blood stone so that its other stones are unfamiliar to most people. This is a great pity for the Changhua stone. Due to the rise of Balindong stone in recent years, the Changhua stone regained attention. Changhuadong stones include Huangdong, Niujiaodong, Yangzhidong, Zhushadong, Wucaidong, Oufendong, Taohongdong, Yunaodong, Mamaodong, Yuzidong, Xuehuadong, etc., all being high-quality stones. Some contain cinnabar and look brightly

red, which are also called chicken-blood stones. Though less bright than the Changhua stone in color, they boast purer and more lovely bases, hence the saying “blood color in south and ground in north.”

The Balin stone comes from Balin, Chifeng, northern China’s Inner Mongolia Autonomous Region. Large-scale exploitation began in 1973. Despite a newcomer for seal cutting, it is now one of the four most famous stones in China. Over the past two decades, Balin has produced many famous stones. More importantly, the Balin stone is under planned, protective exploitation by Balin Stone Group, so that it can escape the irrational extraction that the Shoushan, Qingtian and Balin stones suffered these years and write down a glorious chapter in the history of Chinese seal stones. The mineral contents of the Balin stone are mainly dickite and kaolinite.
