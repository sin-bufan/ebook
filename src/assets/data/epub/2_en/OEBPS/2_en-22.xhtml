Epigraphy and Seal Cutting

By Yao Chunmao

Chinese seal cutting has enjoyed a long, brilliant history, with two peaks in the ancient Zhou, Qin, and Han dynasties and then again in the more recent Ming and Qing dynasties, respectively. From the Zhou Dynasty through the mid-Ming Dynasty, bronze seals dominated. Most seals were cast in metal, with only a few made of ivory, bamboo, wood and jade. With the introduction of Qingtian, Shoushan and other stones during the latter half of the Ming Dynasty, however, things changed radically. With a hardness index set at 2-3, seal stones are easy to carve, and even men of letters could carve seals by themselves. The adoption of stone set a new trend of seal making amongst scholars.

Along with the popularity of seal carving in the Ming

Dynasty, the art became a unique genre integrating calligraphy, engraving and carving. Luminous and smooth, seal stones are easy to engrave. The origination and development of carving techniques injected endless vitality into the ancient art.

In the Ming and Qing dynasties, most seal stones came from Qingtian and Shoushan. Although Changhua chicken blood stone was already famous at the time, it was mainly used as ornaments rather than practical use. In the mid-Ming period, Wen Peng made great contribution to seal cutting by introducing the Qingtian stone, also known as pyrophyllite. It is a solid, delicate mineral with unitary texture and hardness. Due to its affordability, the stone has become the first choice for seal cutters. Typically, the Qingtian stone is mono-colored, but demonstrates a sense of calmness and elegance with its pure, moderate quality. In the late Ming Dynasty, Dongguangdong was the most famous subcategory of the Qingtian stone, which became rare in the early Qing Dynasty.
