Balin Stone: A Precious Treasure

By Bi Shicai

There is a sacred mountain called Tenigurtu (meaning “Place with Transparent Stones” in Mongolian) in Daban Town, capital of Balin Right Banner in Chifeng City. Around the mountain is fertile grassland dotted with red flowers. They together compose enthralling scenery. The colorful Balin stone is reserved and produced in this wonderland.

Archeological findings in Chifeng and Balin Right Banner (Inner Mongolia) indicate that ancestors of local people gave birth to the brilliant jade and stone culture of the Chinese nation. Of a number of jade relics unearthed there, many were made of Balin stone. For instance, the 8,000-year-old Human-Face-Shaped Jade Ornament and the 5,000-year-old Bird-Shaped Jade Disc, Round Jade

Disc, Jade Silkworm and Jade Melon, as well as jade beads, cups and bowls from the Xia, Shang, Zhou, Qin and Han dynasties have all been proven to be made of Balin stone, despite the fact they are called jadeware. From the Tang to Qing Dynasty, jade and stone culture was further classified, and “jade-like stone” became an individual category. Balin stone was then categorized as “heaven-born stone or mysterious stone”. The Qidan tribe originating in the Balin Grassland, founders of the Liao Dynasty beyond the Northern Song Dynasty, used Balin stone to carve seals and make porcelain and Buddhist statues. Many relevant relics have been unearthed in recent years. In the Yuan Dynasty, Genghis Khan called the stone “Tengriqolu” (meaning “Heaven-given precious stone” in Mongolian). At the time, helmets, cups and bowls made of the Balin stone were popular among the royal family and aristocrats. Qing Emperor Kangxi built a temporary palace in Daban Town, and the Balin stoneware creation became increasingly prosperous.
