The history of Shoushan Stone Carving can be traced back more than 200 years. It began with the round-carved figures by Yang Yuxuan and the Bogu-style seal knobs by Zhou Shangjun. Later, Lin Qingqing invented the Boyi style that features a strong scholarly atmosphere. The carving was then divided into Dongmen and Ximen genres. Throughout history, its carving techniques have been passed down by word-of-mouth-to-mouth through generations. In terms of carving styles, it is now categorized into Round Carving and Boyi. Its subjects involve mythical animals, figures, fruits, and patterns, among many others. The carving has survived the test of time and won wide favor. Inheritance does not mean to accept all traditional rules, but respect the traditions while practicing all basic skills. One may form a distinctive

personal style and seek innovation only after gaining sufficient insight into the traditions. Innovation evolves through inventions and changes based on mastery of all basic techniques and deep understanding of the unique style and traditional culture and spirit of the craft. In regard to Shoushan Stone Carving, successful innovation should take root in the following three points: First, absolute respect for the beauty of stone; second, preservation of traditional cultural symbols and introduction of new ones; finally, balanced and harmonious expression of final work.

One must endure loneliness as a collector of Shoushan stones. Collection aims to preserve the cultural essences created in the history of human civilization, and collectors need profound cultural accomplishment and insightful mind, as well as wisdom gained through undergoing vicissitudes in life. The value of the Shoushan stone lies in its beauty bestowed by both Mother Nature and the efforts of artists over ages. At first sight of the stone, one may be impressed by its gentleness.
