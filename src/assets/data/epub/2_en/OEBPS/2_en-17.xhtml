In the reign of Emperor Yongzheng, seal knob-carving further matured both in terms of subjects and technique, reaching its peak in dexterity, vivid designs, and exquisite craftsmanship. Emperor Yongzheng himself made tremendous contributions to the development of seal knob carving. Though his reign lasted for only 13 years, a huge number of masterpieces were created thanks to imperial enthusiasm for art, strict pursuit for perfection, and remarkable artistic accomplishment. According to historical documents in the Palace Museum in Beijing, the Qing court left behind more than 8,000 stone seals (including those granted to officials or scattered amongst ordinary people), of which nearly 60 percent were made during the reign of Emperor Yongzheng. Mr. Qi Lan, a scholar in Shanghai, has estimated that, on average, one or two seals were brought into the imperial storehouse each day during the reign of Emperor Yongzheng, many of which were personally reviewed by the emperor or

made upon his decree. The seal knobs, stone carvings, and arhat statues housed in the Palace Museum in Beijing and the National Palace Museum in Taipei indicate that masterpieces from the time of Emperor Yongzheng reflect the aesthetic tastes and artistic accomplishments of the Qing Dynasty at its peak. Pictures of those masterpieces remain important references for current research. Emperor Qianlong had a wide range of interest and thus focused less on stone seals than Emperor Yongzheng. So, few classic seal knobs were created during his time. In the middle of the early Qing Dynasty, stone materials, techniques and aesthetics were fully integrated into the cultural ecosystem of the upper class. The strong national strength and creative energy were mirrored on the making of every small seal knob, not only creating a number of classic masterpieces, but also making it an independent genre of art.
