The Inheritance of Seal Knob Culture

By Pan Jingshi

Seal knob carving is a time-honored traditional art. Maturing from the late Ming Dynasty to the early Qing Dynasty, it has a history of some 400 years as an independent genre. The art is highly associated with the aesthetic tastes of scholars. Through analyzing seal knobs carved in the time of Emperors Kangxi, Yongzheng and Qianlong, one can sense the changes in the aesthetic vogue of scholars in different eras.

In the early Qing Dynasty, seal knob carving was dominated by the aesthetics of scholars in areas south of the Yangtze River, influenced by the methods and styles of inkstone, bamboo, wood, and ivory carving. Also, it was also influenced by the aesthetic styles of scholarly landscape painting and flower and bird painting

of the late Ming and early Qing period. For this reason, an overwhelming majority of seal knobs of the time reflect the styles of relief carvings of landscapes, flowers and birds and line carvings. In the early Qing Dynasty, the style of royal seal knobs was actually in line with scholarly aesthetic tastes at that time, and drew inspiration from their works in terms of subject, technique and expressive form. The government seal cutting department directly collected and purchased seals from ordinary people, which were further imbued with the indescribable elegance coming from the areas south of the Yangtze River.

Seal knob carving reached its pinnacle during the golden period that lasted for some 150 years from the reign of Emperor Kangxi (1662-1722)through the reign of Emperor Qianlong (1736-1795), and many seals made in the period have been considered masterpieces by later generations.
