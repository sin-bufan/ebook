Delight in Shoushan Stone

By Ye Shaobo

Shoushan stone is a kind of natural stone produced in central Fujian Province. From the Qing Dynasty, it won great favor from men of letters, and those of top quality even became imperial tributes. As a sort of clay mineral rich in silicon and aluminum, its main mineral ingredients include dickite, kaolinite, and nacrite. With a hardness of 1.5-3 on the Mohs scale, it is easy to carve. The earliest Shoushan stone carving appeared in the Southern and Northern Dynasties (420-589). At the time, the stone was not regarded too highly and merely used to carve simply-crafted figures and animals as burial objects. In the late Yuan and early Ming Dynasty, some scholars began to use stones to cut seals. Historical records show Wan Mian was the first to carve seals with the “Huaru stone”. Although no verdict has been pronounced on what the Huaru stone

exactly is, this is still widely considered a milestone event marking the introduction of stone in seal-cutting. More specific records show that Wen Peng was the first to use Qingtian stone. Later, it gained popularity amongst seal cutters due to its advantages in color and texture, becoming a mainstay material for seal-cutting. Because such stone is easy to carve and demonstrate calligraphers’ unique brushwork, it was highly rated by scholars in the Ming and Qing dynasties. Some even claimed that the jade-like stone was costlier than real jade. Afterwards, the natural beauty of stones was gradually discovered and appreciated by more people, giving birth to various genres of stone carvings with regional characteristics.

The beauty of the Shoushan stone lies in its texture.
